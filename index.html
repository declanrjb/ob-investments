Oberlin College Invested $1.2 Million in Israeli Companies, Investigation Finds

An investigation by The Review identified more than $52 million of Oberlin College’s investments across Europe, Asia, and the Middle East.

By Declan Bradley | May 1, 2026

In the 2021-22 fiscal year, fund managers employed by Oberlin College invested more than $1.2 million in Israeli companies, a Review investigation found. The findings come in the wake of two student petitions for divestment from Israeli companies and repeated campus protests since 2023.

The Review’s investigation identifies more than $52 million of the College’s investments in 69 companies around the world. Its publication represents the public’s first access to the details of those investments, which have previously been kept a closely guarded secret of the College’s fund managers.

The four Israeli companies – Fireblocks, Mobilesson, Orca Security, and Cyber Hunters – are all part of the Israeli tech sector. Two – Orca and Cyber Hunters – specialize in cybersecurity. Mobilesson Ltd, which does business under the name ConnectTeam, makes a brand of HR productivity software. Fireblocks maintains software infrastructure for cryptocurrency and digital financial transactions, largely serving as a backend for other financial technology apps like Revolut.

With $477,298 worth of transferred stock, Fireblocks ranked third among the College’s top foreign investments in the 2021-22 fiscal year. Mobilesson ranked eighth, with $384,713 of purchased stock.

Twice, in April 2024 and April 2025, Oberlin students have filed petitions calling on the Board of Trustees to divest from Israeli companies. The Board has rejected calls for divestment on both occasions, and has never publicly acknowledged any such investments.

In its response to the 2025 divestment proposal, the Board cited grounds for rejection including its fiduciary responsibility to maintain the financial health of the endowment and a desire to prioritize engagement with world affairs over disengagement. The Board also cited the college’s use of capital funds to manage its investments.

“The endowment does not invest directly in individual companies, ” the Board wrote in its August 2025 response. “Instead, it invests in externally managed investment funds (e.g., mutual funds, private equity funds, venture capital funds) selected on the strength of the fund manager, who in turn invests in companies. Oberlin has practically no influence over the fund manager’s investment decisions. Oberlin’s only practical decision is whether to invest in or avoid a fund.”

The Review’s investigation draws on previously unreported documents uncovered in a publicly available database maintained by the Internal Revenue Service (IRS). The documents, which appear as an appendix to the College’s 2021 990-T nonprofit income tax return, include 154 pages of disclosures filed under Treasury regulations 1.351-3(a) and 1.6038B-1(c), which require nonprofits to disclose significant investments in foreign-controlled entities.

Company names and mailing addresses for the four Israeli companies reported in the documents match those appearing in articles of incorporation filed with the Israeli Business Registry.

The possible existence of Israeli investments has remained a contentious issue on Oberlin’s campus for more than twenty years. In 2002, Oberlin Students For a Free Palestine (SFP) published an open letter calling on the Board to “to make public its investments and financial holdings in Israel,” but did not receive any such disclosures. In 2015, the Board rejected an SFP proposal for divestment from Israeli companies, citing insufficient evidence that the student body was united on divestment as a course of action.

The College’s investment portfolio gained renewed attention following the October 7, 2023 Hamas attacks on Israel. In March 2024, representatives of the Oberlin SFP and Jews for a Free Palestine (JFP) met with then Board Chair Chris Canavan, OC ‘84, along with representatives of the College and the Board’s Investment Committee, to discuss a renewed student proposal for divestment from Israeli companies. At that time, both Canavan and Oberlin Chief Financial Officer Jun Yang described themselves as unable to disclose the College’s investments due to agreements with the College’s fund managers, who consider such details their intellectual property.

In October 2025, a student protest for divestment outside a Board of Trustees meeting at the Oberlin Hotel escalated to a physical confrontation with Oberlin PD. Students who attempted to enter the building were met by uniformed officers who responded with pepper spray. At least one student was later taken to the hospital with a concussion.

The incident followed a previous divestment protest in December 2024, during which students attempted to gain access to a Board of Trustees meeting taking place on the second floor of the Hotel. Students attempting to enter the Board’s meeting space were confronted and stopped by campus safety officers.

Israeli companies made up about 8% of the College’s foreign investments in the 2021-22 fiscal year. The documents detail investments in a total of 69 companies managed through 27 investment funds, totaling a combined $14 million of purchased stock. The College invested an additional $38 million directly in seven investment funds, five of them based in the Cayman Islands.

Software and tech ventures led the College’s portfolio, with more than $3 million invested in companies in that sector. A further $1.4 million went to cryptocurrency and stock trading platforms, and $1.2 million to companies that develop mobile apps or games. Three of the College’s investments that year – ADA Support ($192K), Synthesia Limited ($167K), and Svava ($133K) – now market generative AI services as their primary product.

In June of 2021, the College’s fund managers purchased a $873K stake in Paper Education Company, Inc – a Canadian startup that promised to provide K-12 students with one on one virtual tutoring. The company has since been accused of misleading customers, after an investigation by Chalkbeat showed that tutors spent much of their time working with multiple students at once as a cost-saving measure.

Among the College’s other notable investments are the period-tracking app Clue ($161K) and Rei Do Pitaco Limited ($160K) – a Brazilian sportsbetting app.

The College’s fund managers invested heavily in companies in Europe, with more than $8M distributed to 35 companies in that region. North and South America followed, with a combined $3.5M invested across 14 companies.

A complete database of Oberlin’s foreign investments in the 2021-22 fiscal year is available at oberlinreview.org.
